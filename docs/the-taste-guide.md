# The Taste Guide

## What Taste Is

Taste is the ability to distinguish good from great, and to consistently make choices that result in quality.

It is not subjective preference. "I like blue" is preference. "This blue doesn't work here because it competes with the CTA and confuses the hierarchy" is taste.

Taste is built through three things:
1. **Exposure** — Seeing a lot of good work (and bad work, and knowing the difference)
2. **Analysis** — Understanding WHY something works, not just that it does
3. **Practice** — Making choices, getting feedback, refining judgment

## How to Develop Taste

### Study Good Work
Look at award-winning work (Awwwards, FWA, SiteInspire) but don't copy it. Ask:
- What makes this feel different from everything else?
- What's the one design decision that carries the most weight?
- What did they leave out?
- How does the typography create hierarchy?
- How does spacing create relationships?

### Study Bad Work
Equally important. Look at generic templates, AI-generated designs, and corporate sites. Ask:
- Why does this feel generic?
- What decisions were avoided (defaulted to)?
- Where did they use decoration instead of solving a problem?
- What would happen if you removed 50% of the elements?

### Build Your Reference Library
Collect examples of work you admire. Organize by what specifically you admire about them:
- Typography excellence
- Color usage
- Layout innovation
- Interaction design
- Brand coherence
- Writing quality

### Practice Making Calls
Taste develops through decisions, not observation. For every design you work on:
- Make a choice and articulate why
- Get feedback from someone with strong taste
- Revise based on the feedback
- Reflect on what you learned

## How to Encode Taste for AI

The skills and rules in this system are taste encoded as instructions. Here is how to add your own:

### Turn Judgments into Rules
"This doesn't feel right" is not a rule. Break it down:
- "The heading and body text are the same weight, so there's no hierarchy" → Rule: Use at least 2 differentiation methods between hierarchy levels.
- "The spacing feels random" → Rule: Use a consistent spatial scale (base-4).
- "The copy sounds like a robot" → Rule: No sentences starting with "It's important to note that."

### Turn Expertise into Skills
Document what you know in a format AI can use:
- Specific patterns (do this, not that)
- Decision frameworks (when to choose A vs B)
- Quality criteria (how to judge if something is good)
- Common mistakes (what to avoid and why)

### Turn Quality Standards into Agents
Define what a specialist would check:
- What do they look at first?
- What do they never forgive?
- What do they celebrate?
- How do they give feedback?

## The Taste Audit

The taste-audit skill provides a structured scoring system. Use it as a final gate, but remember: taste can't be fully reduced to a checklist. The checklist catches the obvious problems. The rest requires judgment.

The score tells you if you're in the ballpark. Your eye tells you if you've arrived.
